In many ways, the Internet has accelerated people’s notion that the truth is relative. As 
Anna Kata argues, the Internet has ① f acilitated a postmodern paradigm of truth in which 
expert consensus is delegitimized and personal values and experiences are treated the 
same as scientific evidence. Within this new paradigm, Kata notes, everyone is considered 
an expert and truth is “flattened,” as each perspective and view of the facts is 
② represented  as legitimate, with evidence from legitimate experts considered one of 
multiple opinions. The ③ f ixed nature of what is considered true is problematic in terms 
of misperceptions, as people are increasingly exposed to online discussions and debates 
about what is true. If extremely inaccurate evidence, personal experiences, or the words 
of charlatans are presented and considered alongside evidence from scientific consensus 
without consideration of validity based on evidence, it becomes more 
④ dif ficult for 
people to determine which information is legitimate and true. Furthermore, online 
postmodern discourses often shift the standards of evidence to
