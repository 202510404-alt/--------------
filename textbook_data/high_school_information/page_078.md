In the Physics, Aristotle asks whether nature acts for the sake of something. He offers 
an account of rain as water that rises when heated by the Sun, then cools, condenses, and 
falls to Earth, but is not particularly concerned with explaining rain as such. Rather, he 
addresses a more general question: how to account for natural objects, events, and 
processes that appear with regularity. He queries whether the sharpness of front teeth and 
the bluntness of molars is coincidental and rejects that possibility, because this

The sharpness of front teeth allows them to tear while the bluntness of those in the back 
enables them to crush food; both are useful for eating. In Aristotle’s view, all natural 
things happen in a given way and occur for the sake of something; that is the final cause.
