children, upon entering school for the first time, ② h ave  already watched more hours of 
television than the total number of hours of classroom instruction they will encounter in 
their entire school careers. 
③ Add  to this the number of hours that some will have spent 
playing computer games or surfing the Internet. Baudrillard refers to this social creation 
as hyperreality — a situation 
④ which  the simulation of reality is more real than the thing 
itself. For Baudrillard, everyday life has been captured by the signs and symbols  
⑤ generated  to represent it, and we ultimately relate to simulations and models as if they
