Given that sport fans feel successful when their team is successful, the reverse occurs 
when they must deal with the disappointment of scandals and poor team 
performances. 
Sport fans can develop a psychological attachment to sports, teams, and players. This 
connection can assist fans to develop a feeling of being strong, important, and successful.  
( ① ) The term vicarious achievement refers to a sense of accomplishment that is felt 
secondhand, through the success of someone else. ( ② ) Some fans may experience an 
increase in self-esteem when their team is winning. ( ③ ) Others may feel a sense of 
confidence and skillfulness by learning team statistics and club history. ( ④ ) In these 
situations, fans may be a little less eager to publicly announce their support of the team, 
and they might even downplay the failure of the team. ( ⑤ ) Fans can compensate for a 
team’s poor performance by developing strong feelings of closeness with fellow fans, 
being critical of other successful teams, voicing criticism of umpiring or refereeing 
decisions, or by being blindly optimistic that things will get better.

to mentally “hear” the words that the instructor said within the preceding two or three

As we go through life, our brains seem to create a very temporary record of much or all of 
what we sense in our immediate environment. ( ① ) They do so through several sense-
specific areas of the brain that might collectively be called a sensory register. ( ② ) For 
example, if you have ever played with a lighted sparkler on a festive holiday evening — as 
many people in the United States do on July 4th — then you’ve undoubtedly noticed the tail 
of light that follows the sparkler as you swish it about. ( 
③ ) That tail of light isn’t “out 
there” in your environment; instead, this is your sensory register at work, telling you where 
the light has very recently been. ( 
④ ) And think about what happens when you find yourself 
daydreaming during a classroom lecture. ( ⑤ ) As is true for the sparkler’s tail, those words 
aren’t lingering in the air around you; they’re in your head — more specifically, in your 
sensory register.
