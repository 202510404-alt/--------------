There are a growing number of AI tools that companies use to screen job applicants. 
These automated systems decide which smaller set of applicants from a large pool will 
reach a human hiring manager. The AI tool analyzes video interviews for content, word 
choice, facial expressions, and so on, assigning each interviewee an “employability 
score,” which is compared to those of other applicants. But the validity of these tools is 
dubious; in fact, there is no evidence that these scores accurately predict job performance. 
Critics worry that algorithms trained on limited data will be more likely to give higher 
employability scores to typical applicants who are more frequently represented in the 
current workforce and give lower scores to those who appear atypical because they are 
less frequently represented in the current workforce. The result would be to reinforce 
current demographic imbalances instead of building a diverse workforce. Put differently, 
these automated technological tools seem to be reproducing biased wallpaper in hiring 
decisions. And, in fact, the technology may even be magnifying bias in new ways.
