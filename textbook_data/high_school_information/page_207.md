Language is a cultural resource available for people to use. We do things with words, as 
the philosopher J. L. Austin reminded us decades ago. Even when we speak or write to 
ourselves, our very choices of words, as well as our underlying intentions and desires, are 
influenced by the social contexts in which we have seen, heard, or experienced those 
words, intentions, and desires before. Linguistic anthropologists, therefore, maintain that 
the essence of language cannot be understood without reference to the particular social 
contexts in which it is used. But those contexts do not stand apart from linguistic practices
